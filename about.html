Our Story

Built by Someone Who Has
Sat in Your Chair.

Marcel Headlam spent years inside corporate organisations before he built On The Buzzer. He knows what it feels like when the format fails the room. He also knows why games — real ones, with stakes and energy — never do.

The Problem

He Was Not an Entertainer.
He Was an Attendee.

Before On The Buzzer existed, Marcel sat through the same events most people sit through. The icebreakers nobody wanted to do. The team activities that divided rather than connected. The formats that worked brilliantly for six people and collapsed at sixty.

He was not watching from the outside. He was in the room, feeling what everyone else felt.

What struck him was not just that the formats were bad. It was that games — real ones, with competition and stakes — never had that problem. Nobody checks their phone during a penalty shootout. Nobody disengages from a buzzer going off at the wrong moment. Games have always brought people together without anyone having to be told to participate.

That is where The Everyone Show came from. Not from a production company. From someone who felt the problem firsthand — and knew what already worked.

The Solution

What He Built and Why It Works.

The Everyone Show is not a quiz night with better production. It is a structured competitive format designed specifically for corporate rooms — groups where seniority, tenure, and office politics are all present in the same space.

Every round was designed to neutralise those dynamics. Speed on the buzzer does not care about job title. Strategic wit is not reserved for senior leadership. The format levels the room by design, not by instruction. That is not an accident. It is the whole point.

Most formats assume the problem is engagement. It is not. The problem is design. When the structure is right, engagement follows automatically. You do not have to coax a room into enjoying a penalty shootout. The Everyone Show works on the same principle.
